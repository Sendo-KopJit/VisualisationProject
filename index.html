Design Principle

Just a colour key
for Minkowskis3D

Every accent colour maps to a spacetime concept. Timelike intervals are blue, spacelike are red, lightlike are cyan, the same visual language across every tool, so the interface teaches physics as you explore it.

ds² = (c·Δt)² − Δx² − Δy² Minkowski interval
ds² > 0  →  TIMELIKE future cone interior
ds² < 0  →  SPACELIKE outside light cone
ds² = 0  →  LIGHTLIKE on the cone surface
τ = ∫√(ds²) / c proper time

Minkowski Spacetime Explorer

Draw worldlines in the 2D cross-section editor and explore them in interactive 3D spacetime. The reference light cone at the start event defines causal structure, segments are coloured by whether they are timelike, spacelike, or lightlike. Sixteen presets from inertial motion to Rindler acceleration, with a full observer-frame animation mode.

Open Simulation ↗
How to use
1

Open the simulation using the button above or by clicking the preview. It opens in a new tab.

2

Load a preset: click the ✎ Draw Path button at the bottom, then select the Presets tab for ready-made worldlines.

3

Or draw your own: on the x-ct tab, draw upward with your mouse to define spatial motion over time. Use y-ct to add depth.

4

Apply and start: click ✓ Apply to 3D, then press ▶ Start in the sidebar to animate the observer along the worldline.

5

Watch proper time τ accumulate on screen. Segments coloured blue are timelike, red spacelike, cyan lightlike.

6

Orbit the view by dragging the 3D canvas. Scroll to zoom. Adjust pitch and yaw in the sidebar sliders.

Snell's Law & Total Internal Reflection

Drag the incident ray to set the angle of attack. Adjust n₁ and n₂ to switch between any pair of optical media (air, glass, water, diamond). Watch the refracted ray bend according to Snell's law, see Fresnel reflection and transmission coefficients update live, and cross the critical angle to trigger total internal reflection with its decaying evanescent field.

Open Simulation ↗
How to use
1

Drag the ray tip: the glowing yellow dot at the end of the incident ray. Moving it left or right changes θ₁, the angle of incidence.

2

Adjust n₁ and n₂ using the sidebar sliders to change the refractive indices of the upper and lower media.

3

Find the critical angle: set n₁ > n₂ (e.g. glass → air) and slowly increase θ₁. The refracted ray bends further until it disappears entirely.

4

Total Internal Reflection: once θ₁ exceeds θ꜀, all light reflects. Watch the evanescent field glow just below the boundary.

5

Drag the boundary up or down to reposition the interface. Use presets in the sidebar for classic scenarios like optical fibre or diamond.

6

Read the Fresnel coefficients R and T in the sidebar: they show how much light is reflected vs transmitted at each angle.

N-Body Gravitational Simulator

Click to place masses anywhere on the canvas, then drag to set initial velocity. The simulation integrates Newton's law of gravitation in real time using a 4th-order Runge-Kutta scheme. Reproduce stable figure-8 choreographies, slingshot trajectories, chaotic three-body divergence, and Lagrange-point libration. Scroll to zoom, right-drag to pan, and watch energy conservation tracked live.

Open Simulation ↗
How to use
1

Click the canvas to place a body at that position. The mass is set by the new mass slider in the sidebar.

2

Click and drag to set an initial velocity. The dashed arrow shows direction and magnitude; release to launch the body.

3

Right-click a body to remove it. Right-click and drag on empty space to pan the view.

4

Scroll to zoom in and out, centred on the cursor. Use the fit button in the sidebar to frame all bodies.

5

Load a preset from the sidebar to instantly set up classic scenarios: figure-8 orbit, solar system, binary star, chaotic 3-body, slingshot, or Lagrange points.

6

Open the theory panel at the bottom of the canvas for the physics behind the simulation, including conservation laws, Lagrange point stability, and what to look for in each preset.

About

About PhysViz

What is this?

PhysViz is a collection of interactive, browser-based physics visualisations, each one a self-contained explorer for concepts that are traditionally hard to grasp from equations alone. The goal is to let you develop genuine physical intuition: draw a worldline and watch proper time accumulate, place charges and see field lines form, set a velocity and observe simultaneity break down. Every tool runs in the browser with no install, no login, and zero external dependencies.

Who made it?

PhysViz is made by Huzaifa Safdar, a Year 12 student with an interest in physics and how it can be visualised. The project is an ongoing effort to build tools that make abstract physics tangible, starting with special relativity and expanding across mechanics, waves, quantum theory, and electromagnetism.
