The Light Clock

Two parallel mirrors with a photon bouncing between them. Each round trip is one tick. In its own rest frame the clock ticks at a constant rate set only by the mirror separation L.

Why the moving clock ticks slower

In the lab frame the moving clock drifts horizontally at speed v. The photon must travel a diagonal path to reach the far mirror — it covers more distance, so it takes longer, even though it still moves at c.

d = 2L  (rest frame, one round trip)
d′ = 2√(L² + (v·Δt′/2)²)  (lab frame)

Deriving γ from Pythagoras

Because d = c·Δt and d′ = c·Δt′, squaring and rearranging gives:

c²Δt′² = 4L² + v²Δt′²
Δt′ = Δt / √(1 − v²/c²) = γ·Δt
γ = 1 / √(1 − β²)   where β = v/c
γ = 1at rest: clocks agree γ = 2β = 0.866: moving clock at half speed γ → ∞β → 1: moving clock frozen

Reading the triangle

The dashed triangle drawn beneath the moving clock: green vertical leg = L, blue horizontal leg = v·Δt/2, orange hypotenuse = c·Δt′/2. The Pythagorean relation between those three lengths is the time dilation formula.
