Module 2

Foundations of Physics

2.1

Physical Quantities and Units

2.1.1

Physical Quantities

a

Physical quantities have a numerical value and a unit.

b

Making estimates of physical quantities listed in the specification.

OCR expects you to estimate sensible values, e.g. speed of a car ~30 m s⁻¹, wavelength of visible light ~500 nm, mass of an electron ~9×10⁻³¹ kg.
2.1.2

SI Units

a

SI base quantities and units: mass (kg), length (m), time (s), current (A), temperature (K), amount of substance (mol).

SI Base UnitDefine · 2.1.2a
A unit from which all other units are derived. There are six assessed at AS level. kg · m · s · A · K · mol — memorise all six Reject: the candela (cd) is the 7th but not assessed. The coulomb and ohm are derived, not base.

MCQ method: identify a base unit

  1. Recall: kg, m, s, A, K, mol.
  2. Reject any unit with a compound name or a slash: $\text{coulomb} = \text{A\,s}$, $\Omega = \text{kg\,m}^2\text{s}^{-3}\text{A}^{-2}$, $\text{V} = \text{kg\,m}^2\text{s}^{-3}\text{A}^{-1}$.
The ampere IS a base unit. The coulomb is NOT.
b

Derived units of SI base units (e.g. momentum kg m s⁻¹, density kg m⁻³).

d

Checking the homogeneity of physical equations using SI base units.

Both sides of a valid equation must have the same base units. If they don't, the equation is wrong.
e

Prefixes: pico (p, 10⁻¹²), nano (n, 10⁻⁹), micro (μ, 10⁻⁶), milli (m, 10⁻³), centi (c, 10⁻²), kilo (k, 10³), mega (M, 10⁶), giga (G, 10⁹), tera (T, 10¹²).

f

Conventions for labelling graph axes and table columns, e.g. speed / m s⁻¹.

2.2

Making Measurements and Analysing Data

2.2.1

Measurements and Uncertainties

a

Systematic errors (including zero errors) and random errors in measurements.

Random vs Systematic ErrorDefine · 2.2.1a
Random error: unpredictable scatter in readings around a true value. Reduced by taking repeated measurements and calculating a mean.

Systematic error: a consistent offset in all readings in the same direction (e.g. zero error on a ruler). Cannot be reduced by repeating — must be identified and corrected. Reject: "human error" — too vague. Name the specific source (reaction time, parallax, zero error).
b

Precision and accuracy.

Precision and AccuracyDefine · 2.2.1b
Precise: readings are close together (small random error / small spread).
Accurate: readings are close to the true value (small systematic error).

A precise instrument is not necessarily accurate (e.g. a poorly calibrated digital balance gives consistent but wrong readings).
c

Absolute and percentage uncertainties when data are combined by addition, subtraction, multiplication, division and raising to powers.

$$\%\text{unc} = \frac{\Delta Q}{Q} \times 100$$ $$\text{Add or subtract: add } \mathbf{absolute} \text{ uncertainties}$$ $$\text{Multiply or divide: add } \mathbf{percentage} \text{ uncertainties}$$ $$Q^n : \quad \%\text{unc}(Q^n) = n \times \%\text{unc}(Q)$$

MCQ method: W = VIt percentage uncertainty (2024)

  1. $\%V = (0.20/12) \times 100 = 1.67\%$
  2. $\%I = (1.0/80) \times 100 = 1.25\%$ (convert mA → A first)
  3. $\%t = (0.01/60) \times 100 = 0.017\%$
  4. Total $= 1.67 + 1.25 + 0.017 = 2.9\%$
Never add absolute uncertainties for multiplied quantities. Always convert units before calculating percentages.
d

Graphical treatment of errors: line of best fit, worst line, absolute and percentage uncertainties from gradients, percentage difference.

$$\text{Gradient uncertainty} = \frac{\text{gradient}_\text{worst} - \text{gradient}_\text{best}}{2}$$ $$\text{Consistency: } \%\text{diff} = \frac{|E_\text{calc} - E_\text{ref}|}{E_\text{ref}} \times 100 \quad \text{Consistent if } \%\text{diff} < \%\text{unc}$$

Exam method: consistency check (2023 Q21c-iii)

  1. Calculate $\%\text{diff} = |1.15 - 1.17|/1.17 \times 100 = 1.7\%$
  2. Compare: $1.7\% < 3.3\%$ (calculated uncertainty).
  3. State: "% difference < % uncertainty, therefore the values are consistent."
Always write the comparison explicitly in words. A number alone scores 0.
2.3

Nature of Quantities

2.3.1

Scalars and Vectors

a

Scalar and vector quantities — give examples of each.

Scalar vs VectorDefine · 2.3.1a
Scalar: has magnitude only (distance, speed, mass, energy, temperature, time, power).
Vector: has magnitude and direction (displacement, velocity, acceleration, force, momentum, weight). Speed is scalar; velocity is vector. Distance is scalar; displacement is vector.
b

Vector addition and subtraction.

c

Vector triangle to determine the resultant of any two coplanar vectors — by calculation or scale drawing.

$$\text{Resultant: draw vectors tip-to-tail; resultant closes the triangle from tail to tip.}$$
d

Resolving a vector into two perpendicular components.

$$F_x = F\cos\theta \qquad F_y = F\sin\theta$$
$\theta$ is always measured from the reference direction (usually horizontal). Horizontal component uses cos, vertical uses sin.

Module 3

Forces and Motion

3.1

Motion

3.1.1

Kinematics

a

Displacement, instantaneous speed, average speed, velocity and acceleration.

Key kinematic quantities3.1.1a
Displacement: distance in a specified direction (vector).
Speed: rate of change of distance (scalar).
Velocity: rate of change of displacement (vector).
Acceleration: rate of change of velocity (vector). $a = \Delta v / \Delta t$.
b–d

Graphical representations: displacement–time (gradient = velocity), velocity–time (gradient = acceleration, area = displacement).

$$s\text{-}t\text{ graph: gradient} = v \qquad v\text{-}t\text{ graph: gradient} = a,\quad \text{area} = s$$
For non-linear graphs OCR expects you to estimate the area by counting squares or using the trapezoid rule.
3.1.2

Linear Motion (SUVAT)

—
$$v = u + at \qquad s = ut + \tfrac{1}{2}at^2 \qquad v^2 = u^2 + 2as \qquad s = \tfrac{(u+v)}{2}t$$ $$\text{Free fall from rest: } u=0,\ a=g=9.81\ \mathrm{m\,s^{-2}},\quad v=\sqrt{2gh},\quad g=\frac{2s}{t^2}$$

Exam method: distance from v-t graph (3 marks, 2022 Q21a)

  1. Split the area under the graph into simple shapes (trapezoids, rectangles, triangles).
  2. Trapezoid (0–3 s): $s_1 = \frac{1}{2}(4.0 + 10.0)\times 3.0 = 21\ \mathrm{m}$
  3. Rectangle (3–8 s): $s_2 = 10.0\times 5.0 = 50\ \mathrm{m}$. Total $= 71\ \mathrm{m}$.
C marks are awarded for each correct partial area — an incorrect total can still score if the method is right. Always show the separate areas.

Exam method: find g from drop-time data (2024)

  1. Inspect all readings; if one is clearly different, state it is anomalous and exclude it.
  2. Calculate mean $t$ from remaining values.
  3. Substitute into $g = 2s/t^2$.
Must identify the anomaly before excluding it. Silently discarding it loses the mark.
3.1.3

Projectile Motion

a–b

Independence of vertical and horizontal motion. Two-dimensional motion: constant horizontal velocity, constant vertical acceleration $g$.

$$\text{Horizontal: } x = v_x t \qquad \text{Vertical: } y = \tfrac{1}{2}gt^2\ (\text{from rest})$$

Exam method: projectile landing check (2023 Q24b)

  1. Horizontal distance $= v_\text{rebound} \times t = 3.6 \times 0.58 = 2.09\ \mathrm{m}$
  2. Acceptable range: centre $\pm$ radius $= 2.0 \pm 0.15\ \mathrm{m}$.
  3. State explicitly: $1.85\ \mathrm{m} < 2.09\ \mathrm{m} < 2.15\ \mathrm{m}$ — ball lands in ring.
The numerical comparison to the target range must be written. A distance alone scores 0.
3.2

Forces in Action

3.2.1

Dynamics

a–c

$F = ma$. Weight $W = mg$. Forces: tension, normal contact, upthrust, friction.

$$F = ma \qquad W = mg \quad (g = 9.81\ \mathrm{m\,s^{-2}})$$

Exam method: resolve tension to find resistance force (3 marks, 2022 Q21b)

  1. Read acceleration from v–t graph gradient: $a = \Delta v / \Delta t = 6.0/3.0 = 2.0\ \mathrm{m\,s^{-2}}$.
  2. Resolve the known force (tension) into the direction of motion: $T_x = T\cos\theta = 680\cos55° = 390\ \mathrm{N}$.
  3. Apply Newton's 2nd law: $T_x - R = ma \Rightarrow R = 390 - 150\times2.0 = 90\ \mathrm{N}$.
If you give both horizontal and vertical components, it must be clear which one you are using in $F = ma$. The mark scheme penalises ambiguity.
e

Free-body diagrams: show all forces on a single object, labelled with type and magnitude.

Forces on a free-body diagram must be drawn from a single point or the surface they act on. Never draw forces from other objects.
3.2.2

Motion with Non-Uniform Acceleration (Drag and Terminal Velocity)

a–d

Drag as the frictional force through a fluid. Factors affecting drag: speed, cross-sectional area, shape, fluid viscosity. Motion falling in gravitational field with drag. Terminal velocity.

Terminal velocityDefine · 3.2.2d
The constant velocity reached when the resultant force on a falling object is zero — because weight equals the sum of air resistance and upthrust. Reject: "forces balance" alone. Must say "resultant force = 0". Reject: "velocity stops increasing" without the force reason.

Exam method: explain terminal velocity (3 marks, 2024)

  1. Mark 1: weight = air resistance (+ upthrust).
  2. Mark 2: resultant force $= 0\ \mathrm{N}$.
  3. Mark 3: acceleration $= 0$, so velocity is constant.
"Forces are balanced" alone scores 0. Never say velocity decreases at terminal velocity.
3.2.3

Moments, Couples and Equilibrium

a–f

Moment $= Fx$. Couple: torque $= Fd$ (pair of equal, opposite, parallel forces). Principle of moments. Centre of gravity. Equilibrium under forces and torques. Triangle of forces.

$$\text{Moment} = F \times d_\perp \qquad \text{Torque of couple} = F \times d$$ $$\text{Equilibrium: } \sum F = 0 \text{ and } \sum M = 0$$ $$\text{Hanging picture: } W = 2T\cos\theta \quad (\theta \text{ from vertical})$$

Exam method: explain why bending knees reduces spine force (3 marks, 2024)

  1. The perpendicular distance between the line of action of the weight and the hips (pivot H) is reduced.
  2. Therefore the moment about the hips is smaller.
  3. So the compressive force on the spine is reduced.
Do not mention centre of mass. Do not use knees as pivot — it is always the hips.
Two-force equilibrium MCQ (2023 Q16): Only "forces are equal and opposite" is true. Newton's 3rd law pairs act on different bodies — so two forces on the same object are NOT a 3rd law pair. An object moving at constant velocity is also in equilibrium. Answer: A (Only 1).
Balanced rod / inverse proportionality MCQ (2022 Q15, Answer D): Principle of moments: $F \times x = W \times d_W$ (constant). So $F \propto 1/x$ — $x$ is inversely proportional to $F$. The graph of $F$ vs $x$ is a hyperbola, not a straight line. Upward force at pivot $= F + W$ (not just $F$). The moment of $W$ is constant, not $Fx$.
3.2.4

Density and Pressure

a–c
$$\rho = \frac{m}{V} \qquad p = \frac{F}{A} \qquad p = h\rho g$$ $$\text{Upthrust} = \text{weight of fluid displaced} = \rho_\text{fluid}\,Vg = mg_\text{displaced}$$
Upthrust MCQ: only "mass of water displaced × g" is physically and dimensionally correct. "Density of object × g" is a trap.
3.3

Work, Energy and Power

3.3.1–3

Work, KE, GPE and Power

—
$$W = Fx\cos\theta \qquad E_k = \tfrac{1}{2}mv^2 \qquad E_p = mgh$$ $$P = \frac{W}{t} = Fv = F\cos\theta\cdot v$$ $$\text{Efficiency} = \frac{\text{useful output energy}}{\text{total input energy}} \times 100\%$$
Derive $E_k = \frac{1}{2}mv^2$ from first principles using $v^2 = u^2 + 2as$ and $F = ma$, or via work done: $W = Fs = mas = m \cdot \frac{v^2}{2s} \cdot s = \frac{1}{2}mv^2$.
3.4

Materials

3.4.1

Springs

a–d

Tensile/compressive deformation. Hooke's Law. Force constant $k$. Force-extension graphs.

$$F = kx \qquad E_\text{el} = \tfrac{1}{2}Fx = \tfrac{1}{2}kx^2 = \text{area under }F\text{-}x\text{ graph}$$
Compressing or extending both increase stored elastic PE. The direction of displacement does not matter.

Exam method: verify force constant in lab (2 marks, 2022 Q22a)

  1. Add a range of known loads (forces/weights) to the spring and measure the compression (or extension) for each load.
  2. Plot a graph of force against compression. The gradient equals the force constant $k = F/x$.
Do not write "length" for compression — OCR requires the change in length (compression/extension), not the absolute length. Allow $W = mg$ for the force. Allow $k = F/x$ but $k$ must be the subject.

Exam method: elastic PE calculation (2 marks, 2022 Q22c)

  1. Identify the compression $x = x_\text{max} - x_\text{natural} = 0.45 - 0.25 = 0.20\ \mathrm{m}$.
  2. $E = \frac{1}{2}kx^2 = \frac{1}{2}\times 1.7\times10^4\times(0.20)^2 = 340\ \mathrm{J}$.
Ignore $E = \frac{1}{2}Fx$ — OCR explicitly ignores this form. Do not confuse spring compression with platform height above ground.
Energy chain A → B → C (2022 Q22b, 1 mark): From B (moving, maximum speed) to C (stationary, highest point) the energy change is kinetic energy transferred to gravitational potential energy. Not elastic potential energy — the spring is at its natural length at B and C. Ignore thermal losses.
Why the energy equation is wrong (2022 Q22d, 1 mark): The expression $E = \frac{1}{2}mv^2$ omits the gravitational PE gained by the person rising from B to C. The elastic PE stored goes to both KE and GPE — not KE alone.
3.4.2

Mechanical Properties of Matter

a–f

Stress, strain, Young modulus, UTS. Stress–strain graphs for ductile, brittle and polymeric materials. Elastic and plastic deformation.

$$\sigma = \frac{F}{A} \quad (\mathrm{Pa}) \qquad \varepsilon = \frac{x}{L} \quad (\text{no units}) \qquad E = \frac{\sigma}{\varepsilon} = \frac{FL}{Ax} = \frac{kL}{A}$$
Brittle vs Ductile vs Polymeric3.4.2e
Brittle: linear stress–strain graph, fractures abruptly at elastic limit with no plastic region (e.g. glass, ceramic).
Ductile: linear elastic region then large plastic region before fracture (e.g. copper, steel).
Polymeric: non-linear, large extensions at low stress, may show loading/unloading hysteresis (e.g. rubber). MCQ: brittle = straight line ending abruptly. If the graph curves or has a plastic region, it is not brittle.

Exam method: measure cross-sectional area of wire (3 marks, 2023 Q21b)

  1. Use a micrometer to measure the diameter.
  2. Measure in different places along the wire and calculate a mean.
  3. $A = \pi r^2 = \pi d^2/4$.
Must measure in multiple places — wires are not perfectly uniform.

Exam method: derive E = kL/A (2 marks, 2023 Q21c-i)

  1. State all four: $E = \sigma/\varepsilon$, $F = kx$, $\sigma = F/A$, $\varepsilon = x/L$.
  2. Substitute: $E = \dfrac{F/A}{x/L} = \dfrac{kx \cdot L}{A \cdot x} = \dfrac{kL}{A}$.
Do not skip steps — every substitution must be visible on the page.
3.5

Newton's Laws of Motion and Momentum

3.5.1

Newton's Laws of Motion

a

Newton's three laws of motion.

Newton's LawsState · 3.5.1a
1st: An object remains at rest or moves with constant velocity unless acted upon by a resultant force.
2nd: $F = ma$ (net force = rate of change of momentum: $F = \Delta p / \Delta t$).
3rd: When A exerts a force on B, B exerts an equal and opposite force of the same type on A — on a different body. 3rd law: forces act on DIFFERENT bodies. Two forces on the same body in equilibrium are NOT a 3rd law pair.
b–e

Linear momentum $p = mv$ (vector). Net force = rate of change of momentum. Impulse $= F\Delta t = \Delta p$ = area under F–t graph.

$$p = mv \qquad F = \frac{\Delta p}{\Delta t} \qquad \text{Impulse} = F\Delta t = m(v_2 - v_1)$$

Exam method: force from collision with wall (3 marks, 2023 Q24a)

  1. Define positive direction. Ball in at $+7.2\ \mathrm{m\,s^{-1}}$, rebounds at $-3.6\ \mathrm{m\,s^{-1}}$.
  2. $\Delta p = m(v_2 - v_1) = 0.058 \times (7.2 + 3.6) = 0.626\ \mathrm{kg\,m\,s^{-1}}$
  3. $F = \Delta p / \Delta t = 0.626 / 0.052 = 12\ \mathrm{N}$
Missing the sign reversal on rebound halves $\Delta p$. Add the two speeds for a rebound — do not subtract.
3.5.2

Collisions

a–c

Conservation of momentum (no external resultant force). Elastic (KE conserved) and inelastic (KE not conserved) collisions.

Elastic vs Inelastic Collision3.5.2c
Perfectly elastic: total KE is conserved. Momentum is always conserved.
Inelastic: total KE decreases (converted to heat/sound/deformation). Momentum is still conserved.
MCQ: the quantity always conserved in collisions = total momentum. KE is NOT always conserved.

Exam method: Newton's 3rd law from p-t graphs (2 marks, 2022 Q23a)

  1. Mark 1: The gradients of the two p–t lines are opposite in sign (one positive, one negative) — forces on A and B are in opposite directions.
  2. Mark 2: The magnitudes of the gradients are equal — so the forces on A and B are equal in magnitude (both = 24 000 N in 2022).
Simply saying "the forces are equal and opposite" without reference to the graphs scores only 1 mark. Must link to the gradients / rate of change of momentum.

Exam method: show momentum is conserved using a p-t graph (2 marks, 2022 Q23b)

  1. Read momentum values before collision: A $= +20$, B $= -30$. Total $= -10\ \mathrm{kg\,m\,s^{-1}}$.
  2. Read values after collision: A $= -4$, B $= -6$. Total $= -10\ \mathrm{kg\,m\,s^{-1}}$.
  3. Totals are equal — therefore momentum is conserved.
An alternative valid approach: loss of momentum of A $= 24$ kg m s⁻¹; gain of momentum by B $= 24$ kg m s⁻¹ — equal exchange, therefore conserved.

Exam method: velocity of combined objects after collision (2 marks, 2022 Q23c)

  1. Total momentum after $= -10\ \mathrm{kg\,m\,s^{-1}}$, combined mass $= 2.0 + 3.0 = 5.0\ \mathrm{kg}$.
  2. $v = p/m = 10/5.0 = 2.0\ \mathrm{m\,s^{-1}}$ (ignore sign for magnitude).

Exam method: free-fall time after mid-air collision (3 marks, 2022 Q23d)

  1. Objects fall from rest vertically after collision (horizontal velocity is separate and doesn't affect vertical motion).
  2. $s = \frac{1}{2}gt^2 \Rightarrow 120 = \frac{1}{2}\times9.81\times t^2$
  3. $t = \sqrt{240/9.81} = 4.9\ \mathrm{s}$
Allow 4.95 s, but not 5.0 s (insufficient precision). The horizontal velocity does not affect the vertical fall time.

Module 4

Electrons, Waves and Photons

4.1

Charge and Current

4.1.1

Charge

a

Electric current as rate of flow of charge: $I = \Delta Q / \Delta t$.

$$I = \frac{\Delta Q}{\Delta t} \qquad Q = It$$
b–c

The coulomb as the unit of charge. Elementary charge $e = 1.6 \times 10^{-19}\ \mathrm{C}$ (electron = $-e$, proton = $+e$).

d

Net charge on a particle or object is quantised — always a multiple of $e$.

$$Q = ne \quad\Rightarrow\quad n = Q/e = 4.0\times10^{-18}/1.6\times10^{-19} = 25\ \text{electrons lost (2023 Q20)}$$
e–g

Current as movement of electrons (metals) / ions (electrolytes). Conventional current vs electron flow. Kirchhoff's 1st law: conservation of charge at a junction.

Kirchhoff's First Law4.1.1g
The sum of currents entering a junction equals the sum leaving it. This is a statement of conservation of charge. $\sum I_\text{in} = \sum I_\text{out}$
Current from electron flow MCQ (2022 Q10, Answer C): $I = \Delta Q/\Delta t = nq/t = 3.8\times10^7\times1.6\times10^{-19}/(1.2\times10^{-6}) = 5.1\times10^{-6}\ \mathrm{A}$. Watch the $\mu\mathrm{s}$ prefix: $1.2\ \mu\mathrm{s} = 1.2\times10^{-6}\ \mathrm{s}$.
Total energy of N electrons through pd V MCQ (2022 Q18, Answer D): Energy gained by one electron $= eV = 1.60\times10^{-19}\times V$. For N electrons: $E = N\times V\times1.60\times10^{-19}\ \mathrm{J}$. Option C is wrong — it lacks the factor N. Option A has wrong units (V alone is not energy).
4.1.2

Mean Drift Velocity

a–c

Mean drift velocity. $I = Anev$ where $n$ is number density of charge carriers. Conductors, semiconductors and insulators differ in $n$.

$$I = Anev \quad\Rightarrow\quad v_d = \frac{I}{Ane}$$
Number density $n$4.1.2b
The number of free charge carriers per unit volume of a material (m⁻³). It is a material property — it does not change if you change the length or cross-sectional area of the wire. Two copper wires always have the same $n$ regardless of their dimensions.
Series wires MCQ (2023 Q9): same material → same $n$. Same circuit → same $I$. Different area $A$ → different $v_d$ and different $R$. Answer: A (charge carrier density and current equal).
4.2

Energy, Power and Resistance

4.2.2

E.m.f. and p.d.

a–e

Potential difference (p.d.): energy transferred per unit charge between two points. E.m.f.: energy transferred from source per unit charge. Distinction between e.m.f. and p.d. in terms of energy transfer. Energy: $W = VQ = EQ$. Electron acceleration: $eV = \frac{1}{2}mv^2$.

E.m.f. (electromotive force)Define · 4.2.2b
Energy transferred from chemical (or other) energy to electrical energy per unit charge flowing through the source. Units: V or J C⁻¹. OCR wording: "energy transferred per unit charge from chemical to electrical energy" NOT energy transferred to the load. NOT the pd across the internal resistance. NOT "voltage of the cell". EMF = terminal pd only when $I = 0$.
Potential differenceDefine · 4.2.2a
Energy transferred from electrical energy to other forms per unit charge passing between two points. Reject: "voltage across". Use "potential difference" or "p.d.".
Similarity between e.m.f. and p.d. (1 mark, 2022 Q25a): Both are measured in volts / both are measured with a voltmeter / both are defined as energy per unit charge. This is a "state one similarity" question — any one of these three answers scores the mark.
Kirchhoff's laws MCQ (2022 Q2, Answer A): "Which term is NOT used in either of Kirchhoff's two laws?" — Answer: charge. Kirchhoff's 1st law uses current; 2nd law uses e.m.f. and potential difference. Charge is conserved but not explicitly named in the laws.
4.2.3–4

Resistance and Resistivity

—
$$R = \frac{V}{I} \qquad R = \frac{\rho L}{A} \qquad \rho = \frac{RA}{L}$$ $$\text{Gradient of }R\text{-}L\text{ graph} = \frac{\rho}{A} \implies \rho = \text{gradient} \times A$$
Resistivity $\rho$Define · 4.2.4
A material property quantifying resistance to current flow. Units: Ω m. Does not change when you change the dimensions of the wire. Resistance R changes with length and area. Resistivity ρ does not.
Resistance vs resistivity MCQ (2023 Q10): increasing length → R increases. Resistivity is a material property — it stays constant. Answer: C.

NTC thermistor: resistance decreases as temperature increases (negative temperature coefficient).

Exam method: resistivity from circuit data (4 marks, 2022 Q25c-i)

  1. Find p.d. across the wire: $V_\text{wire} = \varepsilon - 2V_\text{lamp} = 14.4 - 2\times6.0 = 2.4\ \mathrm{V}$.
  2. Find resistance of wire: $R = V/I = 2.4/3.0 = 0.80\ \Omega$.
  3. Rearrange $R = \rho L/A$: $\rho = RA/L = 0.80\times0.54\times10^{-6}/25.0$.
  4. $\rho = 1.7\times10^{-8}\ \Omega\text{ m}$.
The two lamps are in series with the wire — do not forget both lamps contribute $2\times6.0 = 12\ \mathrm{V}$ to the voltage drop. Missing this gives $R = 2.8\ \Omega$ and $\rho = 6.0\times10^{-8}\ \Omega\text{ m}$ for ECF marks.

Exam method: drift velocity from $I = Anev$ (2 marks, 2022 Q25c-ii)

  1. Rearrange: $v = I/(Ane)$.
  2. $v = 3.0\,/\,(0.54\times10^{-6}\times8.5\times10^{28}\times1.60\times10^{-19})$.
  3. $v = 4.1\times10^{-4}\ \mathrm{m\,s^{-1}}$.
Convert $A$ to m² before substituting: $0.54\ \mathrm{mm}^2 = 0.54\times10^{-6}\ \mathrm{m}^2$. A power-of-ten error on the area here is not penalised again if it was already penalised in (c)(i).
4.2.5

Power

a–c
$$P = VI = I^2R = \frac{V^2}{R} \qquad W = VIt \qquad 1\ \mathrm{kWh} = 3.6\times10^6\ \mathrm{J}$$
4.3

Electrical Circuits

4.3.1

Series and Parallel Circuits

a–e

Kirchhoff's second law: sum of e.m.f.s around a loop = sum of p.d.s. Series and parallel resistor combinations. Circuits with both series and parallel.

$$\text{Series: } R_T = R_1 + R_2 + \cdots$$ $$\text{Parallel: } \frac{1}{R_T} = \frac{1}{R_1} + \frac{1}{R_2} + \cdots$$ $$\text{Potential divider: } V_\text{out} = \frac{R_2}{R_1 + R_2} \times V_\text{supply}$$
Kirchhoff's Second Law4.3.1a
The sum of the e.m.f.s around any closed loop equals the sum of the p.d.s around that loop. This is a statement of conservation of energy. $\sum \varepsilon = \sum IR$ around any closed loop.
Parallel resistors MCQ (2022 Q7, Answer B): $1/R_T = 1/12 + 1/R = 1/4$. So $1/R = 1/4 - 1/12 = 3/12 - 1/12 = 2/12$, giving $R = 6.0\ \Omega$.
Internal resistance MCQ (2022 Q8, Answer C): "Lost volts" $= \varepsilon - V_\text{terminal} = 1.2 - 0.9 = 0.3\ \mathrm{V}$. Current $I = V/R = 0.9/6.0 = 0.15\ \mathrm{A}$. So $r = 0.3/0.15 = 2.0\ \Omega$.
Power dissipated MCQ (2022 Q5, Answer C): Average KE lost $= \frac{1}{2}m(v_1^2 - v_2^2) = \frac{1}{2}\times1000\times(400 - 225) = 87\,500\ \mathrm{J}$. Average power $= E/t = 87\,500/2.4 = 3.6\times10^4\ \mathrm{W}$.
Parallel resistors MCQ (2022 Q7, Answer B): $1/R_T = 1/12 + 1/R = 1/4 \Rightarrow 1/R = 1/4 - 1/12 = 2/12 \Rightarrow R = 6.0\ \Omega$.

Exam method: thermistor-LED switch-on (4 marks, 2024)

  1. Read LED switch-on voltage from I-V graph (e.g. $3.0\ \mathrm{V}$).
  2. Read $R_\text{therm}$ at threshold temperature from R–$\theta$ graph (e.g. $30\ \Omega$ at $30\,^\circ\mathrm{C}$).
  3. $V_\text{LED} = \frac{55}{55+30} \times 5 = 3.24\ \mathrm{V}$
  4. State: $3.24\ \mathrm{V} > 3.0\ \mathrm{V}$ — LED switches on.
The comparison to the threshold must be written explicitly as an inequality.

Exam method: potential divider V vs x graph shape (2 marks, 2022 Q25b-i)

  1. At $x = 0$ (slider at A): the wire has zero resistance in parallel with R, so all voltage drops across R. But when the wire length is zero, $V_\text{out} = V_\text{supply} \times R/(0 + R) = 1.5\ \mathrm{V}$.
  2. At $x = L$ (slider at B): the full wire resistance equals R, so $V_\text{out} = 1.5\times R/(R+R) = 0.75\ \mathrm{V}$.
  3. The graph starts at 1.5 V when $x = 0$ and falls to 0 V at $x = L$ (wire resistance $\gg R$). Shape: curve of decreasing gradient (because wire resistance increases non-linearly relative to R).
The graph goes from 1.5 V to 0 V, not 0.75 V. Allow a curve of increasing gradient or straight line for the M mark.

Exam method: explain V variation when BC wire is removed (2 marks, 2022 Q25b-ii)

  1. At A ($x = 0$): no wire length in circuit, so $V = 1.5\ \mathrm{V}$ (all e.m.f. across R, since wire has zero resistance in series).
  2. As slider moves toward B: the length of wire AS increases, adding resistance in series with R. Total resistance increases so current decreases.
  3. At B ($x = L$): resistance of wire $=$ resistance of R, so $V$ is shared equally. $V = 0.75\ \mathrm{V}$. (Alternatively: potential divider at B gives $V = 1.5 \times R/(R+R) = 0.75\ \mathrm{V}$.)
This question has BC removed — without BC the fixed resistor R is in series with the section of wire AS, forming a simple potential divider. Do not treat it as a parallel circuit.
4.3.2

Internal Resistance

a–c

Source of e.m.f. with internal resistance. Terminal p.d., "lost volts". Techniques to determine internal resistance.

$$\varepsilon = I(R + r) \qquad V_\text{terminal} = \varepsilon - Ir \qquad \text{"lost volts"} = Ir$$ $$\text{Cells in series: total } \varepsilon_\text{total} = n\varepsilon,\quad r_\text{total} = nr$$
Internal resistanceDefine · 4.3.2a
The resistance of the source itself. It causes energy to be dissipated as heat inside the source, so the terminal p.d. is less than the e.m.f. NOT "resistance inside the cell". Must link to energy dissipation and reduced terminal pd.

Exam method: explain why large internal resistance is undesirable (2 marks, 2024)

  1. More energy is dissipated as heat in $r$ (or: the voltage drop $Ir$ is larger, so terminal pd $= \varepsilon - Ir$ is smaller).
  2. Therefore less power is delivered to the external load.
Saying "the bulb is dimmer" alone scores 0. Must state the physical reason.

Exam method: show P = 3I(ε − Ir) for three cells (3 marks, 2024)

  1. Mark 1 (B): $\varepsilon = Ir + IR$.
  2. Mark 2 (M): Three cells: $3\varepsilon = 3Ir + IR$.
  3. Mark 3 (A): $P = I^2R = I(3\varepsilon - 3Ir) = 3I(\varepsilon - Ir)$.
Every algebraic step must be visible — never skip lines in a "show that" question.
4.4

Waves

4.4.1

Wave Motion

a

Progressive waves. Longitudinal and transverse waves.

Longitudinal vs TransverseDefine · 4.4.1a
Transverse: oscillations perpendicular to the direction of energy transfer (light, all EM waves, waves on strings).
Longitudinal: oscillations parallel to the direction of energy transfer (sound, ultrasound, compression waves). Reject "perpendicular" for longitudinal. Must say "parallel to direction of energy transfer/propagation".

Exam method: explain compressions and rarefactions (3 marks, 2023 Q22a)

  1. Molecules vibrate/oscillate.
  2. The oscillations are parallel to the direction of energy transfer.
  3. This creates regions of high and low pressure — compressions and rarefactions.
Do not say "varying amplitude". Do not say "perpendicular". Must say "parallel to direction of energy transfer" — not just "parallel".
b–d

Displacement, amplitude, wavelength, period, phase difference, frequency, speed. $f = 1/T$. Wave equation $v = f\lambda$.

$$v = f\lambda \qquad f = \frac{1}{T} \qquad I = \frac{P}{A} \propto A^2$$
Deep to shallow water MCQ (2023 Q17): speed decreases, frequency stays constant (set by source), wavelength decreases ($v = f\lambda$, $f$ fixed). Answer: B.
f

Reflection, refraction, polarisation and diffraction of all waves. Diffraction effects significant when $\lambda \approx$ gap width.

Diffraction MCQ: a 3 cm gap → microwaves ($\lambda \approx$ 1–10 cm) are significantly diffracted. Visible light ($\lambda \approx$ 400–700 nm) is not.
Sound vs EM waves of same wavelength MCQ (2022 Q19, Answer B): 3 cm sound and 3 cm EM waves — what is true for both? (A) Polarisation: only EM waves can be polarised, not sound. (B) Stationary waves: both can form stationary waves. (C) Same frequency: sound speed $\approx340$ m/s, EM speed $= 3\times10^8$ m/s, so frequencies differ enormously. (D) Same speed: no, completely different. Answer: B.
Intensity of laser beam MCQ (2022 Q20, Answer B): Intensity $= P/A$ (power per unit cross-sectional area). You need power and cross-sectional area only. Amplitude alone gives relative intensity; frequency and wavelength do not give absolute intensity.
4.4.2

Electromagnetic Waves

a–e

EM spectrum. Orders of magnitude of wavelengths: radio → gamma. Plane polarised waves. Refraction: refractive index $n = c/v$, Snell's law. Critical angle and TIR.

$$n = \frac{c}{v} \qquad n_1\sin\theta_1 = n_2\sin\theta_2 \qquad \sin\theta_c = \frac{1}{n}\ (n_2 = 1\ \text{for air})$$ $$n = 1.5 \implies \theta_c = \arcsin(1/1.5) = 41.8^\circ$$

Polarisation MCQ — two types to distinguish

  1. Unpolarised source, filter rotated (2023 Q13): after the first filter halves intensity, rotating it further does NOT change intensity. Answer: D.
  2. Polarised source (e.g. laptop, 2024 Q4): at 90° dark; at 180° bright again. Sequence: normal → dark → normal. Answer: B.
  3. Polarised filter rotated about horizontal axis (2022 Q12): rotating about the horizontal axis changes the angle between the filter's transmission axis and the polarisation plane. The intensity changes (Malus's Law). Answer: B (intensity changes).
Read carefully: is the source already polarised, or only the filter? Rotating a filter about the axis of the beam (not the horizontal axis) for an unpolarised source does NOT change intensity after the first filter.
Snell's law MCQ (2022 Q3, Answer C): Angles in Snell's law are always measured from the normal, not the boundary. If the diagram labels 50° and 20° from the boundary, the corresponding angles from the normal are 40° and 70°. Apply $n_1\sin\theta_1 = n_2\sin\theta_2$: $1.5\sin70° = n\sin50°$.
UV wavelength MCQ (2022 Q1, Answer D): UV spans roughly $10^{-8}$ to $4\times10^{-7}$ m. Answer D ($2\times10^{-7}$ m = 200 nm) falls in the UV range. A ($3\times10^{-5}$ m) is IR, B ($1\times10^{-10}$ m) is X-ray, C ($4\times10^{2}$ m) is radio.
4.4.3

Superposition and Interference

a–e

Principle of superposition. Graphical methods. Interference, coherence, path difference and phase difference. Constructive and destructive interference. Young double-slit experiment.

SuperpositionDefine · 4.4.3a
When two or more waves meet, the resultant displacement at any point is the vector sum of the individual displacements at that point.
CoherenceDefine · 4.4.3c
Two sources are coherent if they have a constant phase difference and the same frequency. Required for a stable interference pattern. Two sources of the same frequency but varying phase are NOT coherent.
$$\text{Constructive: path diff} = n\lambda \quad (n = 0,1,2,\ldots)$$ $$\text{Destructive: path diff} = (n+\tfrac{1}{2})\lambda$$ $$\text{Young's slits: } w = \frac{\lambda D}{d} \implies \lambda = \frac{wd}{D}$$

Exam method: find λ when given distance to nth fringe

  1. Fringe spacing $w = \text{total distance} / n$. E.g.: central to 6th fringe $= 9\ \mathrm{mm}$, so $w = 9/6 = 1.5\ \mathrm{mm}$.
  2. $\lambda = wd/D = (1.5\times10^{-3} \times 1.5\times10^{-3})/3.5 = 6.4\times10^{-7}\ \mathrm{m}$
Using 9 mm directly as $w$ instead of dividing by 6 gives an answer 6× too large.
What Young's experiment demonstrates: diffraction (at each slit), superposition, wave nature of light. It does NOT demonstrate refraction.
Young's slits fringe spacing MCQ (2022 Q4, Answer B): $w = \lambda D/d$. Largest $w$ needs largest $\lambda/d$ ratio. Calculate for each: A = 450/0.20 = 2250, B = 510/0.15 = 3400, C = 550/0.25 = 2200, D = 610/0.30 = 2033. Answer: B gives the largest ratio.
Phase difference MCQ (2022 Q6, Answer C): 270° phase difference $= \frac{3}{4}\lambda$ path difference $= \frac{3}{4}\times4.0 = 3.0\ \mathrm{cm}$. So $|b - a| = 3.0\ \mathrm{cm}$. Check row C: $|18.0 - 15.0| = 3.0\ \mathrm{cm}$. Correct.
4.4.4

Stationary Waves

a–f

Formation of stationary waves on strings and in air columns. Similarities and differences with progressive waves. Nodes and antinodes. Stationary wave patterns. Fundamental mode and harmonics.

Stationary waveDefine · 4.4.4a
Formed by superposition of two waves of equal frequency and amplitude travelling in opposite directions. Energy is stored, not transmitted. Has fixed nodes (zero amplitude, destructive interference) and antinodes (maximum amplitude, constructive interference). Do not write "superimpose" — OCR ignores this term. Use "superpose" or "interfere".
$$\text{Node spacing} = \frac{\lambda}{2} \implies \lambda = 2d_\text{node}$$ $$v = f\lambda = 2fd_\text{node}$$ $$\text{Example: } d = 0.84\ \mathrm{m},\ f = 200\ \mathrm{Hz} \implies v = 200 \times 1.68 = 336\ \mathrm{m\,s^{-1}}$$

Exam method: explain stationary wave formation — nodes and antinodes (3 marks, 2023 Q22b-i)

  1. Incident and reflected waves interfere / superpose.
  2. Constructive interference (in phase) → maximum amplitude = antinodes.
  3. Destructive interference (antiphase) → zero amplitude = nodes.
Do not write "superimpose". Do not swap nodes and antinodes.

Exam method: explain why nodes are not exactly zero (3 marks, 2023 Q22b-ii)

  1. Amplitude of the wave decreases with distance from the source.
  2. The reflected wave has a lower amplitude than the incident wave.
  3. Incomplete destructive interference — amplitudes do not fully cancel.
Saying "energy is absorbed" alone scores 0. Must link to amplitude difference causing incomplete cancellation.
How to identify a stationary wave (1 mark, 2022 Q24a): The observer sees fixed nodes and antinodes — regions that do not move. Accept "appearance of nodes/antinodes". Ignore "the waves don't move" — OCR does not credit this alone.

Exam method: measure fundamental frequency of stretched wire (2 marks, 2022 Q24b-i)

  1. Method A (signal generator): Connect wire to vibration generator linked to calibrated signal generator. Adjust frequency until a clear stationary wave (fundamental mode) appears. Read frequency from the generator. Reduce background sound level.
  2. Method B (count oscillations): Count the number of oscillations in a measured time. Take many oscillations (e.g. 10) to reduce timing uncertainty. Use a stopwatch or oscilloscope.
  3. Method C (oscilloscope + microphone): Hold a microphone near the wire connected to an oscilloscope. Measure the period $T$ from the trace; $f = 1/T$.
You need both the method AND the improvement (reduce background noise / take many oscillations / use calibrated equipment) for both marks.

Exam method: wavelength, wave speed and uncertainty in T (2022 Q24b-ii)

  1. Wavelength: At the fundamental mode the wire shows one antinode. The wire length $L = \lambda/2$, so $\lambda = 2L = 2\times0.62 = 1.24\ \mathrm{m}$. (Allow 1.2 m.)
  2. Wave speed: $v = f\lambda = 58\times1.24 = 72\ \mathrm{m\,s^{-1}}$. (ECF from your wavelength.)
  3. % uncertainty in $T = 4f^2mL$: $T \propto f^2 \cdot m \cdot L$, so $\%\text{unc}(T) = 2\times\%f + \%m + \%L = 2\times2.5 + 1.0 + 0.5 = 6.5\%$. Absolute uncertainty $= 0.065\times[4\times58^2\times9.7\times10^{-4}\times0.62] = 0.53\ \mathrm{N}$ (to 2 s.f.).
The power of 2 on $f$ means its % uncertainty is doubled. The absolute uncertainty answer must be given to 2 s.f. only.
4.5

Quantum Physics

4.5.1

Photons

a–e

Particulate nature (photon model) of EM radiation. Photon as a quantum of energy. $E = hf = hc/\lambda$. The electronvolt. Using LEDs to estimate Planck constant $h$.

PhotonDefine · 4.5.1b
A discrete packet (quantum) of electromagnetic energy. Its energy is $E = hf$, directly proportional to frequency. It has zero rest mass and travels at $c$ in a vacuum. Reject: "particle of light" without mentioning energy or frequency.
$$E = hf = \frac{hc}{\lambda} \qquad h = 6.63\times10^{-34}\ \mathrm{J\,s}$$ $$1\ \mathrm{eV} = 1.6\times10^{-19}\ \mathrm{J} \qquad \text{LED: } eV_0 = hf \implies h = \frac{eV_0}{f}$$ $$\text{Plot }V_0\text{ vs }f\text{: gradient} = h/e \implies h = \text{gradient} \times e$$
The cardboard tube in the LED experiment excludes ambient light, making it easier to judge the exact switch-on point — reducing uncertainty in $V_0$.
4.5.2

The Photoelectric Effect

a–f

Photoelectric effect. One-to-one photon–electron interaction. Einstein's equation. Work function, threshold frequency. $KE_\text{max}$ independent of intensity. Rate of emission proportional to intensity (above threshold).

Work function $\phi$Define · 4.5.2c
The minimum energy required to release an electron from the surface of a metal. $\phi = hf_0$ where $f_0$ is the threshold frequency. NOT "energy to remove an electron from an atom". Electrons in metals are already delocalised — the work function is the energy needed to escape the surface.
$KE_\text{max}$Define · 4.5.2e
The maximum kinetic energy of electrons emitted from the metal surface. Depends on frequency of incident radiation only — not on intensity. $hf = \phi + KE_\text{max}$
$$hf = \phi + KE_\text{max} \qquad hf_0 = \phi \qquad KE_\text{max} = hf - \phi$$ $$\text{Intensity} = \frac{P}{A} = \frac{\dot{n}\cdot hf}{A}$$
Increasing intensity (above threshold): more photons/second → more electrons/second → higher current. $KE_\text{max}$ is unchanged because it depends only on frequency.

Exam method: explain why frequency must exceed minimum (3 marks, 2023 Q23b)

  1. Light is made of discrete packets of energy called photons.
  2. An electron is emitted only if the photon energy is $\geq$ the work function.
  3. $E = hf$ — so frequency must exceed the threshold $f_0 = \phi/h$.
Do not mention intensity here. One photon interacts with one electron.

Exam method: current ∝ intensity (3 marks, 2023 Q23c-i)

  1. Intensity $\propto$ rate of incident photons (at above-threshold frequency).
  2. Each photon interacts with one electron (one-to-one) → more photons → more electrons per second.
  3. Current $= \dot{n}_e \cdot e$ → current $\propto$ photon rate $\propto$ intensity.

Exam method: gold leaf electroscope falls (3 marks, 2024)

  1. Electrons on metal surface absorb photons in a one-to-one interaction.
  2. Electrons are emitted from the metal plate.
  3. Electroscope loses negative charge → leaf falls.
Do not say leaf gains positive charge. Electrons come from the metal plate, not the gold leaf.

Exam method: below threshold — why no emission (2 marks, 2024)

  1. Photon energy $hf < \phi$ (less than work function).
  2. One photon cannot give an electron enough energy to escape the surface — no emission regardless of intensity.

Exam method: explain why metal C gives no photoelectrons (1 mark, 2022 Q26a-i)

  1. The photon energy at 380 nm $= hf = hc/\lambda = 3.2\ \mathrm{eV}$ (just enough for metal A).
  2. Metal C has work function $\phi = 3.3\ \mathrm{eV} > 3.2\ \mathrm{eV}$ — photon energy is less than the work function, so no photoelectrons are emitted.
Must state that photon energy is less than the work function of C. "The frequency is too low" alone scores 0 — must relate energy to work function.

Exam method: calculate maximum wavelength for photoelectric emission (1 mark, 2022 Q26a-ii)

  1. At threshold: $hf_0 = \phi \Rightarrow hc/\lambda_\text{max} = \phi$.
  2. For metal D ($\phi = 6.4\ \mathrm{eV} = 6.4\times1.6\times10^{-19}\ \mathrm{J}$): $\lambda_\text{max} = hc/\phi = (6.63\times10^{-34}\times3\times10^8)/(6.4\times1.6\times10^{-19}) = 190\ \mathrm{nm}$.
Allow 194 nm from direct calculation. Remember to convert eV to J before dividing.

Exam method: de Broglie wavelength of photoelectron (3 marks, 2022 Q26a-iii)

  1. Find $KE_\text{max}$: $hf = \phi + KE_\text{max} \Rightarrow KE_\text{max} = 5.3 - 4.1 = 1.2\ \mathrm{eV} = 1.92\times10^{-19}\ \mathrm{J}$.
  2. Find electron speed: $\frac{1}{2}mv^2 = 1.92\times10^{-19} \Rightarrow v = 6.5\times10^5\ \mathrm{m\,s^{-1}}$.
  3. de Broglie: $\lambda = h/(mv) = 6.63\times10^{-34}/(9.11\times10^{-31}\times6.5\times10^5) = 1.1\times10^{-9}\ \mathrm{m}$.
Work function must match the metal being used (metal B = 4.1 eV here, not the threshold metal A). Alternatively use $p = \sqrt{2mKE}$ then $\lambda = h/p$.

Exam method: determine work function from $KE_\text{max}$–$f$ graph (3 marks, 2022 Q26b-ii)

  1. Draw line of best fit through the data points (not forced through the origin or through any specific point like 0.5/5.0).
  2. Calculate gradient of best-fit line: gradient $= \Delta KE_\text{max}/\Delta f = h$ (Planck's constant). Expected range: $5.8\text{–}6.6\times10^{-34}\ \mathrm{J\,s}$.
  3. Use the equation of the line $KE_\text{max} = hf - \phi$. Find the y-intercept (where $f = 0$): $-\phi = y\text{-intercept}$, so $\phi = -(\text{y-intercept})$. Expected: $2.4\text{–}3.0\times10^{-19}\ \mathrm{J}$.
You must use your calculated gradient value (not the given $h$) to find the y-intercept via the equation of the straight line — otherwise the M mark is not awarded.
4.5.3

Wave–Particle Duality

a–c

Electron diffraction — evidence for wave-like behaviour of particles. Diffraction through polycrystalline graphite by atomic spacing. de Broglie equation.

$$\lambda = \frac{h}{p} = \frac{h}{mv}$$ $$\text{Electron diffraction: } \lambda \approx d\ (\text{atomic spacing}) \implies \text{significant diffraction}$$
Wave–Particle Duality4.5.3
All particles have an associated wavelength (de Broglie wavelength). EM radiation behaves as waves (interference, diffraction) and as particles (photoelectric effect). Electrons behave as particles (deflected by electric fields) and as waves (electron diffraction). Evidence for wave nature of electrons: diffraction pattern when electrons pass through graphite. Evidence for particle nature of light: photoelectric effect.
